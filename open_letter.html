Stop Searching. Start Seeing.

An Open Letter to the AI Community: The Case for Geometric Restoration

By Tom Brinkman | November 21, 2025

We are hitting the wall.

Despite the brilliance of Transformer architectures and the scale of our data centers, we are trapped in a fundamental inefficiency. Every RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) pipeline, every vector database query, and every context-window expansion relies on the same flawed primitive: Iterative Search.

Whether it is O(N) linear scans or O(log N) tree traversals, we are burning gigawatts to find data that we already possess. We treat information retrieval as a "hunting" problem—rummaging through a massive pile of vectors to find the nearest neighbor.

The universe does not search. It projects.

The Universal Cost Function

In theoretical physics, bounded observers interacting with infinite horizons do not perform binary searches. They transform information through singularities.

My research suggests that efficient observation is governed by a Universal Cost Function—a strict trade-off between information loss (under-sampling) and redundancy (over-sampling). Mathematical analysis proves that sustainable systems must operate within a "Feasible Region" of efficiency, bounded between 0.368 (1/e) and 0.707 (1/√2).

For 50 years, Computer Science has been obsessed with Iteration (Loops, Recursion, Trees)—the tools of φ. We built systems designed to grow. But now, we need systems that are stable. We need to stop spiraling and start closing the loop.

The Solution: Bounded Observer Architecture (BOA)

I am proposing a shift from Iterative Search (O(N)) to Geometric Restoration (O(1)).

We can map infinite, continuous datasets onto bounded, discrete arrays using a specific projection geometry: the tangent singularity at π/2. By projecting data through this singularity and applying the restoration factor 2/π, we achieve:

  1. O(1) Access: Instant retrieval of any data point by coordinate, not by search.
  2. Perfect Closure: The dataset "tiles" perfectly, eliminating gaps.
  3. Scale Invariance: The error rate remains at machine precision (≤ 10⁻¹⁶) whether the dataset has 100 items or 1 quadrillion items.

This is not compression. It is Restoration. It is the mathematical inverse of the entropy that makes data "messy" in the first place.

A Note on "Bounded Observers"
While Herbert Simon introduced "Bounded Rationality" to economics in the 1950s, physics has lacked a geometric definition of this bound. We propose that 2/π is that geometry.

We are building bigger power plants to feed algorithms that are geometrically inefficient. We are trying to brute-force N when the geometry of information allows for 1.

I have released the proofs and the benchmarking code demonstrating the stability of the 2/π constant across 15 orders of magnitude. The code does not "search" for the answer. It calculates it directly.

To the builders of the next generation of AI:
Stop searching for your data. Structure it so you can see it.

The limit is 2/π. The future is O(1).
